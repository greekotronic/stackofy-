Creatine has a branding problem. For decades it has been marketed exclusively as a supplement for bodybuilders - something you take to lift heavier and recover faster. The fitness industry captured it early and never let go.

The reality is considerably more interesting. Creatine is one of the most universally present compounds in human biology, found in every tissue that has high energy demands: muscle, yes, but also brain, heart and retina. It is an endogenous molecule - your liver, kidneys and pancreas synthesize it from arginine and glycine. You are not taking something foreign. You are giving your body more of something it already knows exactly how to use.

And in 2025, the research on creatine for brain health, cognitive aging and neurodegeneration has reached a level of evidence that makes ignoring it increasingly difficult to justify.

How Creatine Works - The ATP Buffer

Creatine's primary mechanism is elegantly simple. Inside cells, creatine combines with phosphate to form phosphocreatine - a high-energy reserve molecule. When a cell needs ATP faster than it can produce it through normal metabolic pathways, phosphocreatine donates its phosphate group to ADP to instantly regenerate ATP.

Think of it as a cellular energy capacitor. Normal metabolism is like AC power - steady but with a slight lag. Phosphocreatine is the capacitor that handles the sudden power surges - the explosive muscle contraction, the rapid cognitive processing, the burst of neural firing during intense focus. Without adequate phosphocreatine stores, cells hit an energy ceiling sooner and recover more slowly.

As we age, creatine synthesis declines and dietary intake often drops (particularly if meat and fish consumption decreases). The brain - which has among the highest energy demands per gram of any tissue in the body - is particularly vulnerable to this decline.

CABA Trial 2025 - University of Kansas

The Creatine to Augment Bioenergetics in Alzheimer's (CABA) pilot trial tested 20g/day creatine monohydrate in 20 patients with established Alzheimer's disease over 8 weeks. MRI spectroscopy confirmed an 11% increase in brain creatine levels. Cognitive assessments showed a 30% slowing of decline compared to historical controls. The study was designed as a feasibility and safety trial - it was not powered for full efficacy conclusions - but the signal is strong enough to have prompted a larger follow-up trial. The researchers note that standard 5g doses may not reach the brain in sufficient quantities because muscle tissue absorbs creatine first, which is why the higher dose was tested specifically for neurological purposes.

Beyond the Alzheimer's data, a 2025 systematic review in Nutrition Reviews concluded that creatine benefits both muscle and cognition in older adults, with effects on memory, processing speed and mental fatigue being most consistent. A 2024 meta-analysis in Frontiers in Nutrition confirmed processing speed and accuracy improvements in cognitive tasks across healthy adults. And a 2025 trial found that adding 5g creatine daily to cognitive behavioral therapy for depression significantly improved depressive symptoms - consistent with creatine's role in brain energy metabolism and its influence on neurotransmitter systems.

Debunking the Myths

Myth

Creatine damages kidneys

False in healthy individuals. Creatine increases creatinine output (a normal byproduct), which can look concerning on basic labs but does not indicate kidney damage. In people with pre-existing severe renal disease, caution is warranted.

Myth

Creatine causes hair loss

No direct evidence supports this. One 2009 rugby study showed a rise in DHT (a hormone linked to hair loss) - but no hair loss was measured, and no other study has replicated even the DHT finding. The claim lives on in internet forums, not clinical data.

Myth

Creatine causes bloating

Loading phases (20g/day) can cause intracellular water retention - cells draw in water alongside creatine. At a standard 3-5g/day maintenance dose without loading, this effect is minimal and largely imperceptible for most people.

Fact

You need to cycle creatine

False. Creatine works through cellular saturation - the benefits build with consistent use. Cycling resets this saturation and delays results. Daily use is the correct protocol. There is no evidence that the body downregulates creatine synthesis in a problematic way during supplementation.

Fact

You need a loading phase

Only if you want faster results. Loading (20g/day for 5-7 days) saturates muscle stores in about a week rather than the 3-4 weeks it takes at 5g/day. For long-term users, skipping the loading phase and taking 5g daily reaches the same endpoint - just more slowly.

Fact

Expensive forms are better

False. Creatine monohydrate - the cheapest form - has been validated in hundreds of clinical trials. Creatine ethyl ester, buffered creatine and other premium variants have consistently failed to outperform it in head-to-head comparisons. Save your money.

Which Form to Choose

Gold Standard

Creatine Monohydrate

The most studied form. Hundreds of clinical trials. Cheapest per gram. Creapure® is a pharmaceutical-grade version made in Germany with the highest purity certification - worth the small premium if available.

Good Alternative

Micronized Creatine

Standard monohydrate with smaller particle size. Dissolves more easily in water. Gentler on the stomach for people who experience mild GI discomfort. No bioavailability advantage over standard monohydrate.

Skip

Other Forms (HCl, EE, Kre-Alkalyn)

Marketed as superior but consistently fail to outperform monohydrate in head-to-head trials. Significantly more expensive. No evidence of better absorption or efficacy at equivalent doses.

Vegetarians and Vegans: You Need This More Than Most

Creatine is found almost exclusively in animal muscle tissue. Meat-eaters maintain baseline muscle creatine stores through diet; vegetarians and vegans rely entirely on endogenous synthesis, which is insufficient to saturate stores. Studies consistently show larger performance and cognitive benefits from creatine supplementation in vegetarians than in omnivores - because they are starting from a lower baseline. If you do not eat meat or fish, creatine monohydrate is one of the highest-priority supplements you can take.

For cognitive and longevity purposes, any consistent time works. What It Stacks Well With

💪

Protein / BCAAs

Creatine supports rapid ATP regeneration; protein and BCAAs provide the building blocks for muscle repair and synthesis. The most validated combination for physical performance and muscle preservation in aging.

💊

Magnesium

Magnesium is required for ATP synthesis and creatine phosphate reactions. Adequate magnesium ensures the enzymatic machinery that creatine feeds into can actually function. Most people are mildly deficient - a natural pairing.

🔬

ALCAR

Complementary energy support through different mechanisms. ALCAR optimizes fatty acid oxidation in mitochondria; creatine buffers ATP during high-demand bursts. Together they support cellular energy from two distinct angles - no redundancy.

🌿

Taurine

Both support cell volumization and have neuroprotective properties. Taurine's calming effect on the nervous system balances creatine's mildly stimulating energy impact. A useful pairing for cognitive protocols.

🧬

Glycine

Creatine is synthesized from glycine and arginine. While supplemental creatine bypasses the need for endogenous synthesis, adequate glycine supports the broader metabolic context in which creatine operates, including glutathione production.

⚡

CoQ10

Both support mitochondrial energy production. CoQ10 fuels the electron transport chain (steady ATP production); creatine handles burst energy needs. Comprehensive cellular energy support across different timescales.
