Why Long-Term Evidence Is Systematically Missing

One of the book's most striking arguments concerns the structural absence of long-term research - and why that absence is not accidental.

Since vitamin D is a natural substance and cannot be patented, pharmaceutical companies have little financial motivation to invest in such trials. Most existing long-term data comes from publicly funded research or academic institutions, which often lack the scale or resources for comprehensive follow-up.
The Vitamin D Conspiracy - Chapter: Missing Evidence

The book documents that the few long-term studies that do exist consistently show significant protective effects - and are consistently ignored by official guidelines. Two cases stand out.

The Helsinki Study followed participants over 20 years. Those with consistent Vitamin D intake showed significantly lower rates of cardiovascular disease and certain cancers. The findings have received almost no attention in clinical guidelines. The Framingham Heart Study - one of the most influential longitudinal cardiovascular studies ever conducted - found that individuals with low Vitamin D levels had markedly higher heart disease risk. These findings are cited in academic literature but rarely translated into clinical recommendations.

The Ethical Trap

Long-term nutritional studies face a structural ethical problem that conveniently serves the status quo: it is ethically difficult to assign participants to a placebo group with known deficiency over many years. This means the definitive long-term RCT that health authorities demand as proof can almost never be conducted - ensuring the evidence base remains "insufficient" indefinitely. The book argues this creates a permanent barrier to clinical acceptance, regardless of how strong the observational evidence becomes.

How "Negative" Studies Are Designed to Fail

Not all studies showing no benefit from Vitamin D are equally credible. The book documents a pattern of major trials that were widely cited as disproving Vitamin D's benefits - while containing methodological flaws that virtually guaranteed negative results.

Methodology Problem

The VITAL Study (2019)

This large randomized trial investigated Vitamin D supplementation on cancer and cardiovascular outcomes. Media widely reported it found no significant benefit. What was not reported: the study used a fixed dose of 2,000 IU regardless of participants' baseline Vitamin D status - and did not target deficient individuals. Giving an adequate dose to people who are already sufficient and measuring no improvement is not evidence that Vitamin D does not work. It is evidence that the study was not designed to find a benefit.

Methodology Problem

The WHI Study (2006)

The Women's Health Initiative concluded that Vitamin D and calcium supplementation did not significantly reduce osteoporosis risk. What was not reported: the Vitamin D dose used was 400 IU per day - well below the levels consistently associated with therapeutic effect in clinical research. Reporting this as evidence that Vitamin D does not protect bones is equivalent to testing whether antibiotics work by giving half a dose and declaring them ineffective.

The Media-Pharmaceutical Connection

Perhaps the most provocative section of the book examines how the relationship between pharmaceutical advertising and media coverage shapes what health information reaches the public - and what does not.

Pharmaceutical companies rank among the largest advertising clients for mainstream media, and this creates potential conflicts of interest. Coverage of natural, low-cost health interventions like vitamin D tends to receive less attention or is framed in a negative light, while high-revenue pharmaceuticals are frequently promoted or defended.
The Vitamin D Conspiracy - Chapter: How Media and Pharmaceutical Companies Work Together

The book draws on documented cases - GSK and antidepressants, Pfizer's $2 billion advertising spend during the COVID-19 period - not to make conspiratorial claims, but to establish a structural point: when advertising dependency shapes editorial decisions, natural compounds without patent protection are systematically disadvantaged in public conversation.

Dr. Marcia Angell, former editor-in-chief of the New England Journal of Medicine, warned that pharmaceutical funding has compromised the integrity of medical journals. She described how drug companies sponsor clinical studies, influence editorial decisions, and use publication bias to promote favorable outcomes, all while funding journal advertisements and reprint orders to amplify preferred messages.

The History They Don't Teach

One of the book's most compelling chapters reconstructs the early 20th century history of Vitamin D - a history that is both extraordinary and largely forgotten.

The story of Vitamin D is not merely a tale of scientific discovery — it is a reflection of how medicine, politics, and public perception intertwine. Once hailed as a miracle nutrient responsible for eradicating one of the most widespread childhood diseases of the industrial age, Vitamin D fell into relative obscurity by the mid-20th century, only to re-emerge decades later with implications reaching far beyond bone health.
The Vitamin D Conspiracy - Chapter: Suppressed by Design?

By the 1920s, the scientific establishment had effectively solved rickets through Vitamin D supplementation and food fortification. Schools built rooftop solaria. Hospitals integrated sunbathing into pediatric care. The American Medical Association published articles on Vitamin D's power. Then - in the mid-20th century - something changed. The book traces what happened next and why the institutional enthusiasm for Vitamin D was replaced by decades of caution, downplayed research and systematically conservative guidelines.
