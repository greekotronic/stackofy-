Gray Hair

Can You Reverse Gray Hair? What Actually Works

Alethia Research Institute · 17 min read · July 2026
TL;DR
  • A landmark 2021 Columbia University study found that hair graying is genuinely reversible in some people - individual hairs were documented growing back with a dark root after a white tip, correlating with reduced life stress during that window
  • Reversal is only possible while the underlying melanocyte stem cell pool remains intact - once those stem cells are depleted, which happens progressively with age, the graying becomes permanent, which is why reversal is more plausible in midlife than in your 70s or 80s
  • Deficiencies in vitamin B12, copper, vitamin D, iron, and zinc are genuinely associated with premature graying in multiple studies, but dermatologists are direct that there's no scientific evidence taking these as supplements reverses gray hair in people who aren't actually deficient
  • The FTC sued three supplement companies - GetAwayGrey, Rise-N-Shine, and COORGA Nutraceuticals - over unsupported claims that their catalase-based products reverse gray hair, winning settlements against two and a court judgment requiring $391,335 in consumer refunds against the third
  • Catalase is a protein, and no study has ever tested whether orally ingested catalase supplements are even absorbed intact into the bloodstream, let alone whether they reach hair follicles in any meaningful amount - a basic biochemical gap sitting underneath the entire product category
  • Traditional Chinese approaches like black sesame are low-risk but scientifically unproven for hair repigmentation specifically, while its frequent pairing herb, He Shou Wu, carries a real, well-documented risk of acute liver injury and deserves separate, more cautious treatment

Gray hair sits at an interesting intersection for anyone trying to write about it honestly: there's genuinely fascinating, legitimate science behind why it happens and, in some cases, why it can reverse, and there's also a documented, court-adjudicated fraud case built around a supplement ingredient that almost certainly can't do what it's sold to do. Both of these things are true at once, and untangling them is more useful than either dismissing the whole topic as pseudoscience or accepting the marketing at face value.

Why Hair Actually Turns Gray

Hair color comes from melanin, produced by cells called melanocytes that live in a structure at the base of each hair follicle. These melanocytes are themselves replenished by a reservoir of melanocyte stem cells sitting in a "bulge" region beside the follicle - each time a new hair growth cycle begins, some of these stem cells differentiate into fresh, pigment-producing melanocytes. Gray hair happens when this system starts to fail: fewer melanocytes remain active, producing less melanin. White hair, the more advanced stage, reflects a more complete loss of melanocytes in the follicle bulb entirely.

A landmark 2020 study from Harvard stem cell biologist Ya-Chieh Hsu's lab, conducted in mice, found that both physical and psychological stress accelerated this process directly - stress caused the pool of undifferentiated melanocyte stem cells to proliferate and convert into mature melanocytes all at once, in cases of severe stress, rather than gradually as normally intended. This effectively burns through the reservoir faster than it should be used, leaving fewer stem cells available to pigment future hair growth. In Hsu's mouse model, once this stem cell pool had been depleted, the resulting gray hair could not be reversed.

The Genuinely Exciting Finding: Reversal Is Real, Under Specific Conditions

Just a year after Hsu's mouse findings, a different research team produced a result that meaningfully complicated the "once gray, always gray" conclusion, at least for humans, and at least under certain conditions.

The Columbia University Hair-Stress Study
14Volunteers, Ages 9-65
Mitochondria biologist Martin Picard's team at Columbia University collected individual hair strands from 14 volunteers, digitally imaged them, and used image analysis software to precisely map pigmentation patterns along each strand's length, allowing them to correlate specific segments of hair growth with specific windows of time and reported life stress.
ReversalDocumented, Not Assumed
The researchers found and documented actual reversal patterns - hairs with a dark root growing out beneath a previously white tip, correlating with periods of reduced stress in the same individual's reported life circumstances.
Intact
Stem CellsThe Key Requirement
According to Hsu, whose own mouse work initially suggested graying was irreversible, the human reversal Picard's team observed likely occurred because the melanocyte stem cell pool in those specific hairs hadn't actually been depleted - the mature, differentiated melanocytes had simply stopped producing pigment temporarily, a state that can resolve, rather than the stem cells themselves being permanently lost.
Picard himself was direct about the age-dependent limits of this finding: "In midlife, when hair just started graying, it looks like it has the potential to go back. But when people are in their 80s, hair doesn't just go back to being dark again." As melanocyte stem cell populations naturally decline with age regardless of stress, the window for this kind of reversal appears to narrow over time - a genuinely important caveat that separates this real, exciting finding from a promise that stress reduction can undo decades of graying at any age.

What About Nutritional Deficiencies?

Multiple studies have found real associations between premature graying and deficiencies in specific nutrients - vitamin B12, copper, vitamin D, ferritin, calcium, and zinc all show up repeatedly in this research. The mechanistic story for at least one of these makes biological sense: copper is a required component of tyrosinase, the enzyme directly responsible for synthesizing melanin, so inadequate copper could plausibly impair pigment production at a basic biochemical level.

Association Isn't the Same as a Supplementation Cure

It's worth being precise about what this evidence does and doesn't establish, because the distinction matters enormously for anyone considering a supplement based on it. As one dermatologist put it directly: there is no scientific evidence that taking vitamins such as B12, copper and folate can reverse gray hair, even though deficiencies in these have been associated with graying in some studies. If you have a diagnosed, confirmed deficiency in one of these nutrients, correcting it is a reasonable, medically sensible step for your overall health, and it may plausibly help maintain existing pigment production going forward. But taking these supplements without a documented deficiency, hoping to reverse hair that's already turned gray, isn't something the current evidence actually supports.

The Catalase Story: A Real, Adjudicated Fraud Case

This is the part of the gray hair conversation that deserves the clearest, most direct treatment, because unlike most of the supplement categories covered on this site, this one involves an actual government enforcement action with a court judgment attached to it, not just a general absence of supporting evidence.

The FTC vs. Three Named Companies

In 2015, the Federal Trade Commission filed complaints against GetAwayGrey LLC, Rise-N-Shine LLC (maker of "Go Away Gray"), and COORGA Nutraceuticals Corporation (maker of "Grey Defence"), all marketing catalase-containing supplements with claims like "Just two vitamin pills a day can bring back your natural hair color" and "65% of Grey Defence Customers... Reversed Their Grey!" The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection director stated plainly that these companies claimed their supplements could treat gray hair at its roots, but their root problem was a lack of evidence for their claims. GetAwayGrey and Rise-N-Shine settled, agreeing to stop making reversal claims without reliable scientific evidence. COORGA fought the case and lost - a federal judge prohibited the company from making any hair reversal claims without supporting evidence and ordered $391,335 in consumer refunds.

The proposed theory behind these products was that catalase, an enzyme that breaks down hydrogen peroxide, could counteract a buildup of naturally occurring hydrogen peroxide in hair follicles believed to contribute to graying. It's a real enzyme with a real biochemical function - the problem is what happens (or doesn't happen) once it's swallowed as a pill.

A Basic Problem Nobody Seems to Have Tested

Catalase is a protein. Digestion generally breaks proteins down into their constituent amino acids before absorption, which raises a genuinely basic question that appears to have gone untested: does orally ingested catalase survive digestion intact, get absorbed into the bloodstream in a functional form, and somehow reach hair follicles in a meaningful concentration? An independent review of the available clinical literature found no studies attempting to test whether giving people catalase actually returns hair to its previous color or slows graying - the entire commercial category has been built and sold without the most basic efficacy trial having ever been conducted, let alone a study addressing the absorption question first.

Asian Traditional Approaches: Black Sesame and He Shou Wu

Traditional Chinese Medicine has its own long-standing framework for premature graying, generally understood as a sign of depleted "Kidney essence" or insufficient "Liver blood," and two ingredients show up repeatedly in classical formulas addressing it: black sesame (Hei Zhi Ma) and He Shou Wu (Polygonum multiflorum, also called Fo-Ti). These two are frequently combined in traditional preparations, but they deserve genuinely separate treatment, because their risk profiles are meaningfully different.

Plausible, Unproven, Low Risk

Black Sesame

Black sesame seeds contain copper, iron, and vitamin E, giving at least a plausible nutritional rationale for a traditional role in hair pigmentation, and a meta-analysis of 8 controlled trials confirms real, measurable effects on blood glucose. But no study has directly tested black sesame consumption against hair repigmentation specifically - as a whole food, it carries essentially no meaningful safety risk, but the specific graying claim remains traditional rather than clinically confirmed.

Same Claim, Real Safety Concern

He Shou Wu (Fo-Ti)

Used traditionally for the same premature-graying indication, He Shou Wu carries a well-documented, occasionally severe risk of acute liver injury, with case reports including hospitalized patients and, in one documented case, a five-year-old child. The "prepared" form (Zhi He Shou Wu) is considered safer than the raw root, but human clinical evidence for hair benefits remains limited and of poor quality, and long-term safety data is genuinely lacking even for the prepared form.

A Real, Separate Caution Worth Repeating

If you encounter a product combining black sesame with He Shou Wu, a very common traditional pairing, frequently sold together in capsule form, it's worth understanding that you're combining a low-risk food ingredient with an herb carrying genuine, documented hepatotoxicity potential. Periodic liver function testing is a reasonable precaution if you choose to use He Shou Wu specifically, and it's worth discussing with a physician beforehand, particularly if you have any pre-existing liver condition or take other medications processed by the liver.

What's Genuinely Promising, Still Early

A few current research directions deserve honest mention as areas to watch, without overstating where they currently stand. A 2025 study from Nagoya University found that luteolin, a natural antioxidant found in celery, broccoli, carrots, and onions, completely reversed graying in mice, a genuinely striking result, though entirely unconfirmed in humans so far. Separately, a small human trial of a topical alpha-MSH receptor agonist formulation found real, documented reversal of premature graying in treated volunteers, a legitimate, if narrow and early-stage, treatment approach distinct from anything sold as an oral supplement. Researchers are also exploring compounds that block the body's stress response in ways that might protect the melanocyte stem cell pool directly, though these remain experimental and would likely need to overcome unwanted whole-body side effects before becoming a viable treatment.

A Practical Approach

What Actually Makes Sense Right Now

✓If your graying is relatively recent and you're in midlife rather than your 70s or 80s, genuine, evidence-backed reversal through reduced life stress is a real, if modest and individually variable, possibility - this is one of the more scientifically legitimate claims in this entire category.
✓If you suspect a nutritional deficiency (fatigue, other symptoms alongside the graying), get it properly tested and corrected with your physician - this is medically sensible regardless of any hair-color effect, and may plausibly help maintain existing pigment.
✓Black sesame, as a whole food, is a low-risk addition to your diet if you're drawn to the traditional approach, even though direct evidence for hair repigmentation specifically doesn't exist.
✗Don't buy a catalase supplement expecting it to reverse gray hair - no clinical trial has ever tested this claim, and there's a basic, unresolved question about whether oral catalase is even absorbed in a usable form at all.
✗Don't take He Shou Wu without medical guidance and periodic liver function monitoring - the liver injury risk is real, well-documented, and has affected people with no prior liver disease.
✗Don't expect vitamin supplementation to reverse graying if you don't have an actual, confirmed deficiency - the association between deficiency and graying doesn't establish that supplementation beyond normal levels reverses existing gray hair.
