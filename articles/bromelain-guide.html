Bromelain

The Pineapple Enzyme With Actual RCTs Behind It

Alethia Research Institute · 11 min read · June 2026
TL;DR
  • Bromelain is a mixture of proteolytic enzymes from pineapple stem and fruit, studied for inflammation, sinusitis, digestion and post-surgical recovery
  • Unlike most enzyme supplements, bromelain has genuine randomized controlled trials behind several of its claims - not just small open-label studies
  • A classic pediatric trial in 116 children with acute sinusitis found bromelain alone produced faster symptom recovery than standard therapy or the combination
  • A 2025 triple-blind RCT in 70 patients with mild-to-moderate ulcerative colitis found bromelain supplementation improved disease activity and quality of life over 8 weeks
  • Bromelain has a real, well-documented anticoagulant effect - it appears on hospital lists of supplements that increase bleeding risk during surgery
  • The standard advice to stop bromelain at least one to two weeks before any scheduled surgery is not boilerplate caution - it reflects a genuine, multi-pathway effect on clotting

Bromelain is the enzyme responsible for two things most people have personally experienced: pineapple tenderizing meat, and pineapple making your tongue tingle after eating too much of it fresh. Both happen because bromelain is, structurally, a mixture of cysteine proteases that breaks down protein on contact - including, briefly, the proteins on your own tongue's surface before saliva and mucus repair the damage.

That same protein-breaking mechanism is the basis for bromelain's much longer list of therapeutic claims: anti-inflammatory effects, digestive support, wound healing, sinusitis relief, even adjunct cancer research. What sets bromelain apart from most supplements making similarly broad claims is that several of these specific applications have been tested in actual randomized controlled trials, including some in pediatric populations - a level of evidence most enzyme supplements never reach.

What Bromelain Actually Is

Bromelain is extracted primarily from pineapple stem (industrially, since stems are otherwise a processing byproduct) and also occurs in the fruit itself, though at lower concentrations. It is not a single enzyme but a mixture of several proteolytic enzymes plus other compounds, measured in commercial products by Gelatin Digesting Units (GDU) or Milk Clotting Units (MCU) rather than simple milligram weight alone, since enzyme potency varies by extraction batch.

Mechanistically, bromelain's effects fall into two main buckets. The first is straightforward proteolysis - breaking down protein, which underlies its use in meat tenderizing, digestive support, and topical wound debridement. The second is a broader anti-inflammatory and circulatory effect, where bromelain appears to modulate inflammatory cytokines and prostaglandins, and interferes with the blood clotting cascade by inhibiting platelet aggregation and promoting fibrin breakdown. This second mechanism is the basis for most of bromelain's more ambitious therapeutic research - and also the basis for its most important safety caution.

The Clinical Evidence - Where Bromelain Pulls Ahead of Most Enzyme Supplements

Strong Evidence

Acute Sinusitis (Pediatric)

A trial in 116 children under 11 with acute sinusitis found those treated with bromelain alone recovered significantly faster (6.66 days) than standard therapy alone (7.95 days) or the combination - a result the researchers noted replicated earlier findings.

Moderate Evidence

Ulcerative Colitis

A 2025 triple-blind, randomized, parallel-group RCT in 70 patients with mild-to-moderate ulcerative colitis found bromelain supplementation significantly improved disease activity index and quality-of-life scores over 8 weeks compared to placebo.

Moderate Evidence

Chronic Rhinosinusitis

Pilot studies in adults with chronic rhinosinusitis, including patients who had already undergone sinus surgery, report symptom relief and good tissue distribution of bromelain to rhinosinusal tissue after oral tablet dosing over 3 months.

Moderate Evidence

Post-Surgical Healing

A double-blind RCT in periodontal surgery patients found bromelain capsules reduced bleeding on probing and improved gingival index scores compared to placebo four weeks after pocket-elimination surgery, alongside other small trials in dental and minor surgical contexts.

For context, most supplement ingredients never reach a single properly randomized trial of this size. Bromelain having three independent RCTs across three different conditions is genuinely above the typical evidence bar for this category - though each trial is still a single data point that needs replication before being considered definitive.
Why the Sinusitis Data Is the Most Interesting Part

Most supplement research for respiratory or sinus conditions is thin and observational. The pediatric acute sinusitis trial stands out because it directly compared bromelain alone, standard therapy alone, and the combination of both - and bromelain alone outperformed standard therapy alone on recovery time. That is a genuinely informative trial design, not just "bromelain vs. nothing." It does not mean bromelain should replace medical treatment for sinusitis, but it is one of the more rigorously designed pieces of evidence in the entire enzyme-supplement category.

The Bleeding Risk Is Not Boilerplate - It's Mechanistic

Bromelain genuinely slows blood clotting through multiple pathways: it interferes with the clotting cascade, reduces the stability of existing clots, and helps break down fibrin, the protein mesh that holds clots together. This is not a vague "talk to your doctor" caution applied reflexively to every supplement - it is a specific, multi-pathway anticoagulant mechanism that hospitals take seriously enough to list bromelain among supplements that increase surgical bleeding risk. Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center specifically warns against combining bromelain with warfarin or other blood thinners. The standard recommendation is to stop bromelain at least one to two weeks before any scheduled surgery, and to disclose its use to your surgical team even though it is "just a supplement."

What It Stacks Well With

🟡

Papain

Another plant-derived cysteine protease, from papaya. Frequently combined with bromelain in digestive enzyme blends for broader protein-digestion coverage across two complementary plant sources.

🟠

Curcumin

Some research suggests bromelain may enhance curcumin's notoriously poor oral bioavailability, and both share anti-inflammatory mechanisms, making the pairing common in joint-support formulas.

🍇

Quercetin

Both have been studied together in respiratory and sinus-inflammation contexts, including pilot research pairing bromelain with quercetin and other plant compounds for airway inflammation.

🧪

N-Acetylcysteine

Combined as "BromAc" in early-phase clinical research for intraperitoneal use in specific surgical oncology contexts - a specialized medical application, not a general supplement pairing.

🦠

Rutoside

Studied together with bromelain and trypsin in post-surgical dental trials for reducing swelling and pain after procedures like third molar extraction.

⚠️

NSAIDs (Caution)

Not a beneficial combination - both affect bleeding risk and inflammation pathways. Combining bromelain with ibuprofen, naproxen or aspirin increases bruising and bleeding risk and should be discussed with a doctor first.

Before Any Surgery or Dental Procedure

Stop bromelain at least one to two weeks before any scheduled surgery, including dental procedures, and tell your surgical team you have been taking it. This is the single most important practical instruction in this entire article. The anticoagulant effect is real and multi-pathway, not a theoretical supplement-label disclaimer.

Who Should Be Cautious

Avoid bromelain if you take blood thinners (warfarin, heparin), antiplatelet medications (aspirin, clopidogrel, ticagrelor), or NSAIDs regularly, due to documented increased bleeding and bruising risk. People allergic to pineapple, latex, certain pollens, or cross-reactive foods like celery, carrots, rye, and wheat flour should avoid bromelain entirely, since both food and supplement forms can trigger reactions. Bromelain may also alter absorption of certain antibiotics, including amoxicillin and tetracycline, and may potentiate sedatives. Safety in pregnancy and breastfeeding has not been established, and bromelain is generally not recommended for children outside of supervised clinical research settings. Side effects at high doses can include digestive upset, diarrhea, and heavier menstrual bleeding.

7.0/10
Anti-Aging Factor Bromelain earns a solid score for something rare in this category: actual randomized controlled trials across multiple distinct conditions, including a well-designed pediatric sinusitis trial and a 2025 RCT in ulcerative colitis. That is a meaningfully higher evidence bar than most enzyme or anti-inflammatory supplements clear. It loses points because its most powerful mechanism - the anticoagulant effect - is also its biggest liability, demanding real caution around medications and surgery rather than a generic disclaimer. For people without bleeding-risk medications or pineapple/latex allergies, bromelain is one of the better-evidenced enzyme supplements available, provided the surgical timing rule is actually followed.
