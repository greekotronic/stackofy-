How Long Could You Actually Live?

The book opens its scientific argument with a question most people never ask - and the answer is more surprising than most people expect.

Only about 20 to 30 percent of longevity is locked into your DNA. The rest? That's lifestyle, environment, mindset - and yes, nutrients. We've also seen this play out in places like Okinawa, Sardinia, and Nicoya - so-called Blue Zones where people routinely reach 90 or 100 without medical miracles or luxury gyms. They move daily, eat simply, stay socially connected, and experience very little of the chronic inflammation that plagues Western aging. Meanwhile, in laboratories across the world, mice and worms are living 30 to 50 percent longer thanks to simple tweaks: fewer calories, more NAD, better mitochondrial support. Not radical surgeries. Not robot limbs. Just the biochemical equivalent of a little tune-up, again and again.
The $1,000 Longevity Plan - Chapter: How Long Could You Live?

This is the book's foundational argument - and it is backed by decades of epidemiological and laboratory research. The implication is significant: if genetics accounts for only 20-30% of your lifespan, then the majority of what determines how long and how well you live is within your control. Supplements are one tool in that toolkit. Not the only tool - but a meaningful one when chosen with evidence rather than marketing.

What Blue Zone Centenarians Actually Eat

Before diving into supplements, the book grounds its recommendations in something more fundamental: the dietary patterns of people who have already demonstrated that living well past 90 is possible without any of the compounds discussed in the rest of the book.

Imagine a 103-year-old woman in Okinawa who still grows her own vegetables and dances after dinner. Or a shepherd in Sardinia who walks steep hills daily, eats cheese with every meal, and laughs with his neighbors over red wine. They're not calorie-counting. They're not on keto. They've never heard of resveratrol - but they drink it.

Legumes - Daily

Lentils, chickpeas, black beans. Fiber, resistant starch, plant protein. The book calls them "the rockstars of the longevity world" - cheap, powerful and present in every Blue Zone diet.

🥗

Mostly Plants

Not strictly vegan - but plant-forward. Animal products as side notes, not centerpieces. Pasture-raised when consumed. The anti-inflammatory foundation that supplements build on.

🫙

Fermented Foods

Natto, miso, sauerkraut, yogurt. Combined with high fiber intake, these support a diverse gut microbiome that the book links to immune health, mood and chronic disease resistance.

🫒

Healthy Fats - No Fear

Olive oil, nuts, seeds, fatty fish. Saturated fat from natural sources not avoided. Margarine and vegetable oils largely absent. The fat profile that supports brain and hormone health.

🍽️

Hara Hachi Bu

The Okinawan practice of stopping at 80% full. No macro counting, no app, no restriction. Just listening to the body - a habit the book identifies as one of the most consistently life-extending behaviors across cultures.

🚫

Minimal Processing

White flour, seed oils, added sugars and artificial flavors are largely absent from longevity diets. Not because of ideology - because the people who live longest simply never adopted them.

The Real Secret

The book makes a point that gets lost in most longevity discourse: "It's tempting to chase the next miracle food or crash diet, but longevity doesn't work that way. It's the slow, daily habits that shape your future self. The people who live the longest don't overhaul their diet every January. They just eat real food, day after day, decade after decade." Supplements amplify a good foundation. They cannot replace one.
