Vitamin B6

B6: Why Two Health Authorities Disagree by a Factor of Eight

Alethia Research Institute · 16 min read · July 2026
TL;DR
  • In 2023, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) cut its official safe upper limit for B6 from 25mg/day to 12mg/day. The US and WHO still list 100mg/day as safe - eight times higher than the new European figure, for the exact same vitamin
  • B6 is unusual among B-vitamins because it has a well-documented toxicity syndrome - peripheral neuropathy - that mimics the symptoms of deficiency, which is a genuine, decades-old clinical finding, not a fringe claim
  • EFSA itself states it could not establish a lowest-observed-adverse-effect-level from human data - meaning neither the old 25mg limit nor the new 12mg limit rests on a solid randomized dose-response trial, only on case reports and a small number of older studies
  • Critics of the EFSA reduction (including supplement-industry-aligned groups with a direct financial stake in the outcome) argue the opinion leans too heavily on old evidence - but EFSA's actual published opinion cites a broader systematic review, not a single study, so that specific criticism oversimplifies the record
  • Toxicity from food sources of B6 has never been documented - every reported case involves supplements, and the vast majority involve doses far above even the stricter 12mg limit for months or years
  • The honest position: neither 12mg nor 100mg is a number backed by strong RCT evidence. Both are cautious extrapolations from imperfect data, and reasonable regulators have reached very different conclusions from the same underlying evidence

Vitamin B6, or pyridoxine, is a cofactor in over 100 enzyme reactions, most involved in amino acid metabolism, neurotransmitter synthesis, and hemoglobin production. Deficiency is well characterized and includes anemia, dermatitis, glossitis, mood changes, and peripheral neuropathy. So far, this reads like any other B-vitamin covered on this site.

What makes B6 genuinely different is what happens at the other end of the dose range. Unlike B12 or most other B-vitamins, which are considered essentially non-toxic at any oral dose, B6 has a real, well-documented toxicity syndrome - and two respected international health authorities currently disagree by a factor of eight about where the safe ceiling actually sits.

The Number That Split Two Continents

In 2000, the EU's Scientific Committee on Food set a tolerable upper intake level (UL) for B6 at 25mg/day for adults, based on evidence of neurotoxicity from animal and human studies. That number stood for over two decades. In 2023, following a request from the European Commission to re-review several vitamin and mineral limits, EFSA's Panel on Nutrition, Novel Foods and Food Allergens revised it down to 12mg/day - less than half the previous figure.

Same Vitamin, Same Underlying Evidence Pool, Very Different Conclusions
12mgEFSA, 2023
EFSA's revised opinion, based on a systematic review conducted by university researchers in Copenhagen, Oslo, and at the Karolinska Institute, concluded that peripheral neuropathy was the critical effect to guard against, and set the new UL using an uncertainty-adjusted extrapolation - critically, the panel explicitly states it could not establish a lowest-observed-adverse-effect-level from human data.
100mgUS / WHO
The US Institute of Medicine and the World Health Organization have set the tolerable upper limit at 100mg/day for adults - eight times EFSA's figure - based on their own, separately conducted reviews of a broadly similar (though not identical, and less recently updated) evidence base.
Neither of these numbers comes from a large, randomized, dose-ranging human trial specifically designed to find the exact threshold where toxicity begins. Both are extrapolations - one more conservative, one less so - from a mix of case reports, small studies, and animal data. An eightfold disagreement between two credible bodies working from an overlapping evidence base is itself informative: it tells you the underlying data is genuinely too thin to pin down a precise number with confidence, regardless of which side you find more persuasive.

Why B6 Specifically Causes This Problem

Most B-vitamins are cleared efficiently by the kidneys once your tissues are saturated, with no real toxicity ceiling under normal use. B6 behaves differently: at high, sustained intakes, it appears to interfere with its own function in a dose-dependent way - a phenomenon sometimes called "the vitamin B6 paradox," where excess pyridoxine supplementation can paradoxically impair normal B6-dependent enzyme activity, producing nerve damage that looks similar to the neuropathy caused by a genuine deficiency.

Weighing the Critics on Both Sides

The EFSA reduction was immediately criticized by industry-aligned groups, including at least one organization that has publicly described the change as a "slippery slope for free choice and self-care" - language that signals advocacy rather than neutral scientific critique. A common version of the criticism claims EFSA leaned almost entirely on one old 1987 case study.

Checking the Criticism Against EFSA's Actual Opinion

This specific claim doesn't hold up well against EFSA's published 110-page opinion, which describes systematic reviews of the literature conducted by an academic contractor, drawing on both human case data and animal studies (including a 1978 beagle study), not a single 1987 paper. The 1987 study is real and is cited as one relevant human data point among several - but characterizing the entire re-evaluation as resting on it is a simplification that favors the critics' argument more than the actual document supports. This doesn't mean EFSA's number is correct - only that this particular line of attack against it is weaker than it's usually presented.

At the same time, EFSA's own opinion is honest about its limitations in a way that's worth taking seriously: it explicitly states that a lowest-observed-adverse-effect-level could not be established from human data. That's a genuine admission that the underlying evidence doesn't support a precisely calculated number - it supports a cautious range, and EFSA chose to sit near the conservative end of that range. The US and WHO, working from an overlapping but not identical evidence base, chose to sit further toward the permissive end. Both choices are defensible interpretations of thin data; neither is a settled scientific fact.

The Paradox That Makes This Confusing to Diagnose

Same Symptom, Opposite Cause

Both B6 deficiency and B6 excess can present with strikingly similar symptoms, including peripheral neuropathy, numbness, and tingling. This creates a genuinely awkward clinical situation: someone experiencing nerve symptoms while taking a B6 supplement could, in principle, be either under-treating a real deficiency or causing a toxicity - and the fix for one is the opposite of the fix for the other. This is precisely why checking an actual serum B6 or PLP (pyridoxal-5-phosphate) level matters more here than guessing based on symptoms alone, unlike some other B-vitamins where deficiency and excess look nothing alike.

A Practical Quality Checklist

Before You Buy Any B6 Supplement

✓ Check your total B6 intake across all supplements - B-complex formulas, multivitamins, and standalone B6 products can stack up without you noticing, especially if you're in Europe where the new 12mg ceiling is far easier to exceed than the old 25mg one.
✓ Get a serum B6 or PLP level checked if you experience nerve symptoms while supplementing - don't assume it's deficiency or excess without testing, since both look similar.
✓ Food-based B6 intake requires no caution at all - there is no documented toxicity from diet alone, regardless of how much B6-rich food you eat.
✗ Don't assume "under 25mg" is automatically safe just because that was the older EU standard, or that "under 100mg" is automatically safe just because that's the US figure - both numbers reflect genuine scientific uncertainty, not a firmly proven threshold.
✗ Don't take high-dose B6 (>50mg/day) long-term without a clear reason - the case-report evidence for neuropathy at sustained moderate-to-high doses is real, even if it isn't RCT-grade.

Safety Notes

Peripheral neuropathy is the primary documented risk of B6 excess, and it is generally, though not always, reversible after stopping supplementation - recovery can take months. Symptoms include numbness, tingling, and burning sensations, typically starting in the extremities. Anyone taking levodopa, phenytoin, or other medications that interact with B6 metabolism should not adjust B6 intake without medical guidance. If you experience new nerve symptoms while supplementing at any dose, stop and get evaluated rather than assuming it will resolve on its own.

Stacking a standalone B6 supplement on top of a multivitamin and a B-complex is the most common, easily overlooked way people exceed even the more permissive 100mg ceiling.

⚠️

Long-Term High Dose Alone (Caution)

Sustained moderate-to-high doses without a specific medical reason is the pattern most consistently tied to the case reports of neuropathy above.

The score is pulled down by real, unresolved uncertainty at the safety ceiling: two respected health authorities disagree by a factor of eight on the upper limit, both working from case reports and older studies rather than dedicated dose-ranging human trials. Until better RCT-grade dose-response data exists, the most defensible position is modest, standard-range supplementation rather than confident high-dose use in either direction.
