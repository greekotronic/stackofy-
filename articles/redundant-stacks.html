Stack Audit

The Supplement Combinations You Can Stop Buying

Alethia Research Institute · 10 min read · June 2026
TL;DR

Most people are unknowingly doubling up on supplements that hit the same biological targets. Here is what to cut:

  • NR + NMN together - two roads to the same destination; pick one
  • Resveratrol + Pterostilbene at full dose of both - overlapping mechanisms; use lower combined dose
  • Multiple magnesium forms without a reason - except Taurat + Bisglycinat, which is a smart combo
  • Vitamin C megadosing alongside Quercetin - significant antioxidant overlap at high doses
  • CoQ10 + PQQ is NOT redundant - they do different things and belong together

The supplement industry has a simple growth strategy: convince you that more is always better. Add another product. Stack another layer. Cover every angle.

The result is that many people are spending $200-400 per month on stacks where half the products are quietly hitting the same biological targets as the other half. Not dangerous - just wasteful. And in some cases, the redundancy actually crowds out more important gaps in the protocol.

ARI's position is straightforward: a small, well-chosen stack beats a sprawling one every time. Here is where most people are doubling up without realizing it.

The Redundant Pairs

Redundant
NR + NMN (at full dose of both)

NR and NMN are both NAD+ precursors. NR converts to NMN in the body, which then converts to NAD+. They are two routes to the same destination. Taking both simultaneously does not double your NAD+ output - your cells have a ceiling for how much NAD+ they can produce and use, and you hit it with either compound alone at adequate doses.

A 2026 head-to-head clinical trial found NR raised blood NAD+ by 161% versus 67% for NMN at the same gram dose. A 2025 Nature Metabolism study confirmed both compounds approximately double circulating NAD+ after 14 days. Running both at once adds cost but not meaningful benefit beyond what either achieves alone.

What to do instead

Choose one. If you value the longer human evidence base and CD38 inhibition effect, choose NR. If you prefer the more direct pathway and the rapidly expanding 2024-2026 clinical data, choose NMN. Try each for 8 weeks and compare how you feel. Budget for one quality product rather than two mediocre ones.

Redundant at full dose
Resveratrol + Pterostilbene (500mg each)

Resveratrol and pterostilbene are structural cousins that activate the same core longevity pathways: SIRT1 sirtuin activation, AMPK signaling, Nrf2 antioxidant response and mTOR modulation. At full therapeutic doses of both simultaneously, you are paying twice to activate the same switches.

Pterostilbene has roughly 80% bioavailability vs 20% for standard resveratrol, and a half-life 7 times longer. This means a 100mg dose of pterostilbene delivers more active compound to your cells than 500mg of standard resveratrol capsules. Running both at full dose adds expense without proportional benefit.

What to do instead

Use a lower combined dose: 50-100mg pterostilbene + 100-250mg resveratrol. This combination is actually synergistic at these ratios - the resveratrol contributes its broader cardiovascular evidence base while the pterostilbene handles sustained sirtuin activation. Bonus: this pairing also attenuates pterostilbene's potential LDL-raising effect.

Usually redundant
Multiple magnesium forms + without a clear reason

The magnesium market has fragmented into a confusing array of forms - glycinate, malate, taurate, threonate, citrate, oxide - and many people end up taking two or three simultaneously without understanding why. Most of the time, this is redundant: one well-chosen form at an adequate dose covers the bases.

Magnesium oxide has poor bioavailability and is mainly useful as a laxative. Magnesium citrate is decent but mild. The premium forms - glycinate, taurate, threonate - each have distinct advantages, but that does not mean you need all of them.

The one exception: Taurate + Bisglycinat is actually a smart combo

Magnesium Bisglycinat (glycinate) is the superior form for sleep, nervous system support and general absorption. Magnesium Taurate is formulated with taurine and has specific cardiovascular and neurological targeting - and importantly, it contains less elemental magnesium per capsule, so it does not compete for the same dosing ceiling. Together they cover different tissue targets without overlap. This is the one magnesium combination worth keeping. Read the full Magnesium guide for the complete breakdown.

Overlapping at high dose
Vitamin C (2,000mg+) + Quercetin (500mg+)

Both vitamin C and quercetin are powerful antioxidants that operate through overlapping pathways - both scavenge free radicals, both modulate inflammation, and both support immune function. At moderate doses of both, this is complementary. At megadoses of both simultaneously, the antioxidant overlap becomes significant and you are likely not getting proportional additional benefit from the second compound.

There is also a practical interaction: vitamin C modestly improves quercetin bioavailability, which is useful. But taking 2,000mg of vitamin C alongside 1,000mg of quercetin is likely covering the same antioxidant ground twice over at significant expense.

What to do instead

Keep both - but at sensible doses. 500-1,000mg Vitamin C + 250-500mg Quercetin is a well-designed antioxidant pair. At these doses they complement each other beautifully - vitamin C regenerates quercetin after it neutralizes free radicals. Megadosing both is where the redundancy kicks in.

Redundant
Resveratrol + Quercetin (without NMN or NR)

Resveratrol activates sirtuins. Quercetin inhibits CD38, which protects NAD+ from degradation - preserving the fuel sirtuins need. Together with an NAD+ precursor, this is a beautifully coordinated system. Without NMN or NR in the picture, however, you are activating and protecting a pathway that may not have enough NAD+ substrate to run at meaningful capacity.

Quercetin's CD38 inhibition makes most sense when there is NAD+ worth protecting. Taking resveratrol + quercetin without an NAD+ precursor is like tuning a car engine that has almost no fuel in the tank.

What to do instead

Add NR or NMN to the stack. The trio of NAD+ precursor + sirtuin activator + CD38 inhibitor is one of the most well-designed longevity combinations available. Without the NAD+ precursor, you are missing the foundation the other two depend on.

The Combinations That Look Redundant But Are Not

Not Redundant
CoQ10 + PQQ

These two often get lumped together as "mitochondria supplements" and dismissed as redundant. They are not. CoQ10 fuels existing mitochondria - it is essential for the electron transport chain that generates ATP. PQQ stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis - the growth of entirely new mitochondria. One optimizes what you have; the other increases how many you have. This is one of the most synergistic pairings in the longevity toolkit and both belong in a serious energy and anti-aging stack.

Not Redundant
Zinc + Quercetin

Quercetin acts as a zinc ionophore - it transports zinc into cells where zinc exerts its most powerful immune and antiviral effects. These two do not overlap; they cooperate. Quercetin is the delivery mechanism; zinc is the active agent. Neither works as well without the other in this context. This is a genuine synergy, not a redundancy.

Not Redundant
NMN or NR + Resveratrol or Pterostilbene

NAD+ precursors and sirtuin activators are often sold as competing products, but they are actually complementary by design. NR or NMN raises your NAD+ levels. Resveratrol or pterostilbene activates the sirtuins that run on NAD+. One provides the fuel; the other fires the engine. This is arguably the most important combination in the entire longevity supplement space.

Not Redundant
Vitamin D3 + K2

These two are almost always sold together and always should be. Vitamin D3 increases calcium absorption significantly. Vitamin K2 (as MK-7) directs that calcium to bones and teeth, and away from arteries and soft tissue where it causes harm. D3 without K2 at higher doses is not just less effective - it is potentially counterproductive. This is a genuine biochemical partnership, not a marketing bundle.

The ARI Principle

Every supplement you add should either fill a gap in your protocol or amplify something already there through a distinct mechanism. If it does neither - if it simply doubles a pathway you are already covering - that money is better spent on a higher quality version of what you already take, or on closing a genuine nutritional gap. The best stacks are not the longest ones. They are the most intentional ones.
