Tongkat Ali: The Testosterone Herb With a 63% Fake Product Problem

Alethia Research Institute · 19 min read · July 2026
TL;DR
  • A 2022 meta-analysis found Tongkat Ali significantly raised serum testosterone across pooled trials - but the same analysis reported 87% heterogeneity between studies and a statistically significant sign of publication bias, meaning the pooled effect is real but shakier than the headline number implies
  • The independent supplement-evidence database Examine.com reviewed the same body of research and concluded the data "aren't convincing," despite acknowledging the mechanism is biologically plausible
  • The clearest mechanistic story is that Tongkat Ali reduces sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), freeing up already-circulating testosterone rather than manufacturing new testosterone from scratch - which means it likely helps most in older or stressed men with elevated SHBG, and does little for young men with normal hormone binding
  • A peer-reviewed DNA barcoding and HPLC analysis of commercial Eurycoma longifolia products found only 37% were genuinely authentic, with 27% clearly adulterated - a real, independently measured quality crisis that gets far less attention than the testosterone question itself
  • Multiple independent reports have documented Tongkat Ali products contaminated with heavy metals or spiked with undeclared erectile dysfunction medications like sildenafil (Viagra) - a genuine safety issue distinct from simple underdosing
  • One documented case of acute liver injury following supplementation exists in the clinical literature - rare, but a real reason to take product sourcing and quality seriously rather than treating this as a low-stakes herbal purchase

Tongkat Ali has become one of the most visible entries in the current wave of "ancestral" testosterone-support botanicals flooding social media, usually mentioned in the same breath as shilajit, fadogia agrestis, and various maca-adjacent blends. Of that entire cluster, it's genuinely the one with the strongest formal research backing - a real body of randomized trials, systematic reviews, and even a meta-analysis, rather than the thin case-report evidence that props up some of its trending neighbors. That alone makes it worth taking more seriously than the average viral supplement.

But taking it seriously also means being honest about two things the marketing rarely mentions: the testosterone evidence, while real, comes with genuine methodological caveats that change how confidently you should read the headline claims, and there's a documented, independently measured quality-control problem in the commercial supply chain that's arguably a bigger practical risk than anything to do with hormones. This article works through both, because either one alone tells an incomplete story.

What Tongkat Ali Actually Is, and Where It Comes From

Tongkat Ali is the common name for Eurycoma longifolia, a flowering shrub native to Southeast Asian rainforests, particularly Malaysia and Indonesia, where its bitter root has been used in traditional medicine for generations - as a treatment for fatigue, malaria, fever, and, most relevantly here, as a folk aphrodisiac and fertility aid. The root contains a complex mixture of bioactive compounds, most notably quassinoids (responsible for its famously intense bitterness) and eurypeptides, which researchers believe are behind its hormonal effects. Standardized extracts sold commercially are often labeled with ratios like "100:1" or branded names like LJ100 or Physta, indicating how concentrated the extract is relative to raw dried root - a detail that matters more than it might seem, because it directly affects whether a given product could plausibly deliver a clinically relevant dose at all.

The Testosterone Evidence - Real, But With Real Caveats

The most cited piece of evidence for Tongkat Ali's testosterone effects is a 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis pooling multiple randomized and observational trials. On its face, the headline result looks compelling: a statistically significant increase in serum testosterone across the pooled studies. This is the number that ends up on product pages and in social media captions, usually stripped of everything that came after it in the actual paper.

The random-effects model found a significant increase in testosterone levels among men supplementing with Eurycoma longifolia (standardized mean difference 1.352, 95% CI 0.565 to 2.138, p=0.001) - a genuinely large effect size on paper.
87.27%Heterogeneity
The same analysis reported substantial heterogeneity across the included studies (Q=47.15, DF=6, p<0.0001, I²=87.27%) - meaning the individual trials disagreed with each other far more than would be expected from random variation alone, which weakens confidence in treating the pooled number as one clean, consistent effect.
p=0.0243Publication Bias
Begg's test for publication bias returned a low p-value (0.0243), a statistical signal that studies with null or negative results may be underrepresented in the published literature feeding into this meta-analysis - exactly the kind of finding that should make you read the topline effect size with real caution rather than full confidence.
None of this means Tongkat Ali definitely doesn't raise testosterone - it means the specific number being quoted (a large, statistically significant pooled effect) is sitting on top of a body of evidence with real cracks in its foundation: studies that don't agree with each other very well, and a literature that may be missing some of its less flattering results. Examine.com's independent review reached a similarly measured conclusion, describing the overall data as "not convincing" despite finding the underlying biological mechanism plausible - a useful second opinion that lines up with what the statistics themselves suggest.

Beyond the aggregate concerns, one individual trial is worth mentioning on its own merits, because it illustrates the effect size question well: a 6-month, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial in men with androgen deficiency of aging (ADAM) found Eurycoma longifolia increased testosterone levels in almost half of participants - a meaningful minority effect, not a universal one. That detail matters more than it might seem, because it points toward the mechanism that likely explains most of the story.

Why the Effect Is Probably Concentrated in a Specific Group

The clinical literature increasingly supports Tongkat Ali working primarily as a free testosterone liberator rather than a direct testosterone factory. In men with elevated sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) - a protein that binds testosterone and makes it biologically unavailable, which becomes more common with age and chronic stress - Tongkat Ali appears to reduce SHBG binding, freeing up testosterone that was already present but locked away. Typical protocols use around 200mg/day over 4 to 12 weeks to see this effect in aging men with elevated SHBG. This mechanism is meaningfully different from directly stimulating new testosterone production, and it predicts exactly the pattern the research shows: strong response in some men (those with elevated SHBG to begin with), and little to no response in others (younger men with already-normal free testosterone) - a plausible explanation for why the pooled studies disagree with each other so much. It also means Tongkat Ali is not an effective treatment for primary hypogonadism, where the testicles themselves aren't producing enough testosterone in the first place, regardless of how much is bound up in SHBG.

There's reasonably solid supporting evidence for a few related, narrower claims worth naming separately from the general "testosterone booster" framing: several trials show real reductions in cortisol and subjective stress markers during periods of high physiological or psychological stress, and systematic reviews of erectile function scores (IIEF-5) show statistically significant improvement specifically in men with mild-to-moderate baseline erectile dysfunction, generally requiring 8-12 weeks of consistent use to show up. These are more modest, more specific claims than "boosts testosterone," and they happen to be somewhat better supported by the available data.

The Bigger Problem: What's Actually in the Bottle

Here's where the Tongkat Ali story shifts from "an herb with contested but real evidence" to something that deserves considerably more attention than it currently gets. Independent, peer-reviewed laboratory analysis of commercial products has repeatedly found that a large share of what's sold as Tongkat Ali isn't reliably what the label claims - and this is a measured, documented finding, not a vague "quality varies" caveat of the kind that applies to every supplement category.

Only 37% of Tested Products Were Confirmed Authentic

A study using DNA barcoding validated by HPLC analysis tested eleven commercial herbal products labeled as containing Eurycoma longifolia, purchased from retail shops across Malaysia - Tongkat Ali's country of origin, where you'd expect sourcing to be most reliable. The results: only 37% of the tested products were confirmed genuinely authentic by DNA barcoding, while 27% were clearly adulterated, with the remainder falling into more ambiguous categories. This is a peer-reviewed finding using a validated, specific analytical method, not a marketing claim from a competing brand - and it means that even in the plant's home market, buying a product labeled "Tongkat Ali" was, at the time of testing, closer to a coin flip than a guarantee.

The situation gets more concerning once you look past simple authenticity into what some adulterated or contaminated products actually contain. Multiple independent reports and evidence reviews - including Examine.com's own safety assessment - describe commercial Tongkat Ali products found to contain undeclared pharmaceutical compounds, including sildenafil (the active ingredient in Viagra) and its structural analogs, presumably added by manufacturers looking for a guaranteed subjective effect that the herb alone might not reliably deliver. Others have been found contaminated with heavy metals, including lead and mercury - a risk tied to poor sourcing and processing standards rather than anything inherent to the plant itself, but a real risk to the end consumer regardless of the cause. There's also at least one documented case in the clinical literature of acute liver injury following Tongkat Ali supplementation - a rare event, and one that may well be linked to a contaminated or adulterated product rather than pure extract, but it underscores that "natural" and "risk-free" aren't the same thing here.

Why This Matters More Than the Testosterone Debate

Even if you're fully convinced by the testosterone evidence and comfortable with its caveats, none of that matters if the product in your hand isn't genuine Eurycoma longifolia at a real, standardized concentration. A methodologically shaky-but-real effect from authentic extract is a very different proposition than an undeclared pharmaceutical hidden inside a product marketed as an herbal supplement. The adulteration question deserves at least as much attention as the hormone question, and in practice it currently gets far less.

A Practical Quality Checklist

Before You Buy Any Tongkat Ali Product

✓ Look for a standardized, named extract (LJ100, Physta, or a clearly stated extraction ratio like 100:1 or 200:1) rather than an unspecified "root powder" - standardization is at least a signal of manufacturing accountability.
✓ Check for third-party lab testing and published eurycomanone or quassinoid content - products that disclose specific bioactive compound levels are meaningfully more likely to be genuine.
✓ Be suspicious of products priced dramatically below market average - authentic root extract is genuinely expensive to source and process; unusually cheap products are more likely to be diluted or substituted.
✓ If you have elevated SHBG or are an older man with borderline-low free testosterone, this is the population where the mechanism and evidence align best - a more realistic expectation-setter than treating it as a universal testosterone booster.
✗ Don't expect a dramatic testosterone increase if you're young and healthy with normal SHBG - the mechanism suggests you're not the population most likely to respond.
✗ Don't buy unbranded, untested products from marketplaces with no traceability - the documented adulteration rate makes this a meaningfully different risk category than, say, buying an unbranded vitamin C tablet.

Safety Notes

At standardized, properly sourced doses, Tongkat Ali is generally well tolerated in clinical trials, with minimal reported side effects. The real safety concerns documented in the literature relate almost entirely to product quality: heavy metal contamination, undeclared pharmaceutical adulterants (including sildenafil analogs), and at least one documented case of acute liver injury possibly linked to a contaminated product. Avoid use during pregnancy and lactation, as safety hasn't been established. Anyone with liver or kidney disease, prostate conditions, or cardiovascular issues should discuss use with a physician first, and be especially cautious about product sourcing given the documented contamination risks. Given its hormonal mechanism, anyone with a hormone-sensitive condition should also approach supplementation cautiously and discuss it with a doctor beforehand.
