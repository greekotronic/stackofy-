Vegan Supplements in 2026: One Myth Debunked Twice, One Real Win

Alethia Research Institute · 20 min read · July 2026
TL;DR
  • Spirulina still does not provide usable vitamin B12 for humans - it's predominantly "pseudo-B12," inactive cobalamin analogues that can actively worsen B12 status by competing with real B12 for absorption
  • Chlorella is genuinely different from spirulina: independent lab analysis of 57 commercial algae products found chlorella contained mostly physiologically active B12, while spirulina products contained mostly the inactive pseudo-form - but batch variation is large enough that neither should be your sole B12 source
  • The popular claim that vegetarians and vegans get a bigger cognitive boost from creatine supplementation, due to lower baseline muscle creatine stores, traces back to small early studies - the largest, best-designed replication to date (preregistered, double-blind, crossover RCT, n=123) found no such advantage
  • Algae-derived DHA and EPA omega-3 supplements are a genuine, well-evidenced win for vegans - they deliver the same preformed long-chain omega-3s found in fish oil, bypassing the notoriously inefficient ALA-to-DHA/EPA conversion pathway (under 10% for EPA, 5% or less for DHA in most people)
  • Vitamin D3 (not just D2) is now available from a genuinely vegan source - lichen-derived D3 - closing a real gap that used to force vegans to choose the less-effective D2 form or rely on animal-derived D3
  • None of this changes the fundamental, unglamorous basics: B12 supplementation is non-negotiable for vegans, and iodine, zinc, iron, and long-chain omega-3s deserve ongoing attention regardless of which trendy supplement claim is circulating this year

Vegan nutrition has one of the most persistent myth ecosystems in all of supplement science, and it's worth understanding why before getting into the specifics. Back in the 1980s and 1990s, when veganism was still a genuinely fringe choice with almost no institutional research behind it, a handful of advocacy books made confident claims about nutrient sufficiency that were, at the time, more aspiration than evidence. Those books sold well, got quoted, and became load-bearing beliefs in vegan communities well before the actual research caught up. By the time better studies arrived - often decades later - the original claims had already become folk wisdom, repeated by people who'd never read the underlying science and who had every reason, emotionally and socially, to want the reassuring version to be true. That's not a criticism of vegans specifically; it's how misinformation entrenches itself in any community built around a strong identity and a genuine desire to do the right thing. It just happens that vegan nutrition has been a particularly fertile ground for it, because the stakes (avoiding animal products, full stop) and the science (nutrient bioavailability, absorption mechanisms) pull in different directions often enough to create real tension.

Two of the most durable claims from that era - that algae can supply your B12, and that vegans get an outsized cognitive payoff from creatine - are worth a fresh, current look, because both have now been tested more rigorously than they ever were when the claims first took hold. Neither survives the better evidence particularly well. But it would be a lopsided article if it only tore things down, so this piece also spends real time on a case where the vegan supplement world is genuinely ahead of where casual skepticism would expect: algae-derived omega-3, which isn't a workaround or a consolation prize, but a legitimately equivalent source of a nutrient that used to force vegans into a bad tradeoff.

Myth One: Spirulina as a B12 Source

Of all the vegan nutrition myths, this is probably the oldest, and it has been investigated more times, by more independent research groups, than almost any other single claim in this space - which makes it a useful case study in just how long a debunked idea can keep circulating anyway. The claim's origin is understandable: spirulina, a blue-green cyanobacterium sold as a dried powder "superfood," does register on standard lab assays as containing vitamin B12. Early researchers measured it, found a number, and reported that spirulina was a B12 source. The problem only became clear once analytical chemistry improved enough to distinguish what was actually being measured.

What those early assays were picking up wasn't only true, biologically active cobalamin. It was a mix of true B12 and several closely related molecules called corrinoids, or "pseudo-B12" - compounds structurally similar enough to register on the same test, but chemically different enough that human enzymes can't use them the way they use real B12. This is a genuinely different situation from, say, a food simply containing too little of a nutrient to matter. A low-B12 food is merely unhelpful. Spirulina turns out to be actively unhelpful, and the mechanism for why is worth spelling out rather than just asserting.

Worse Than Useless: Active Interference

Pseudo-B12 analogues compete with real vitamin B12 for the same binding proteins and receptors involved in absorption and cellular uptake, without providing any actual cobalamin function once bound. In practice, that means a corrinoid molecule can occupy a receptor site that a real B12 molecule needed, block it, and then simply sit there doing nothing useful - like a key that fits the lock but doesn't turn it, while a working key waits outside. A controlled study measuring this directly found that participants who took spirulina saw their B12 status significantly worsen, not stay flat. This is specifically why it's not recommended to take spirulina alongside a genuine B12 supplement either - the analogues in the spirulina can interfere with absorption of the real vitamin you're deliberately trying to get, undermining the exact thing you're taking the supplement for.

The good news, if there is any in this story, is that not all algae behave the same way, and the differences are large enough to matter if you're determined to explore this category regardless.

Predominantly Inactive

Spirulina

Lab analysis of commercial spirulina products found pseudo-vitamin B12 (the inactive analogue) as the prevailing form, with true active cobalamin representing roughly 30% or less of total measured B12 in earlier assessments.

Mostly Active, But Variable

Chlorella

The same type of analysis found chlorella products contained predominantly physiologically active cobalamin - genuinely different from spirulina - though total B12 content varied enormously between batches, from non-detectable to substantial.

No B12 At All

Seaweed / Nori (Dried)

Direct studies of vegans relying on dried nori and other seaweeds as a B12 source found them inadequate to prevent or correct deficiency - some seaweed contains no B12 whatsoever, contrary to occasional claims.

Only Reliable Sources

Fortified Foods and Supplements

In over 60 years of vegan dietary practice, only B12-fortified foods and direct B12 supplements (methylcobalamin, hydroxocobalamin, or cyanocobalamin) have consistently proven capable of preventing and correcting deficiency.

Chlorella being "mostly active" sounds like it might rehabilitate the algae-as-B12-source idea, but the batch-to-batch variation undercuts that hope in practice. A supplement you can't dose reliably isn't a supplement you can plan a nutrient strategy around, no matter how good the average sample looks in a lab. If you want the full picture on B12 itself - the different chemical forms, how absorption actually works, and why the once-popular fear about cyanocobalamin specifically turned out to be overblown - that's covered in detail in our complete B12 guide.

The Practical Takeaway

If you're vegan, take a real B12 supplement or rely on consistently fortified foods - there is no reliable algae-based or fermented-food shortcut, and chasing one risks leaving a genuine, serious deficiency untreated while feeling falsely reassured by a "natural" label. This isn't a matter of opinion; it's the conclusion of decades of direct testing, and it's precisely the kind of claim where "natural" and "effective" have quietly come apart.

Myth Two: "Vegans Get More From Creatine" - A Claim That Didn't Survive Replication

This second myth is a more interesting case than the first, because unlike the spirulina story, it didn't start as bad science or wishful thinking. It started as a genuinely reasonable hypothesis, built on a real physiological fact, tested in legitimate early studies that found exactly what the hypothesis predicted. It's a useful reminder that a claim can be scientifically respectable at every step along the way and still turn out to be wrong once someone runs a bigger, better-controlled version of the same experiment.

The physiological fact underneath this one is solid: dietary creatine comes almost entirely from meat and fish, so vegetarians and vegans have measurably lower creatine stores in muscle tissue than people who eat animal products. Given that supplementing with creatine has been fairly well studied for muscle performance, and given some suggestive early data on cognition, someone eventually asked the obvious follow-up question - if you start from a lower baseline, would supplementing move you further, since there's simply more room to fill? Early studies answered yes. The most-cited of these, along with a slightly larger follow-up, found that creatine supplementation produced a noticeably bigger improvement in memory and working memory tasks in vegetarians than in meat-eaters. For a while, that became one of the more scientifically defensible-sounding pieces of vegan supplement advice in circulation - not a myth from an old book, but an actual finding from actual peer-reviewed research.

A Plausible Idea That the Best Available Test Didn't Confirm
OriginalSmall Studies
Early studies (Benton & Donohoe, 2010; Rae et al.) found creatine supplementation produced a notably larger memory and cognitive improvement in vegetarians than in meat-eaters - a genuinely reasonable hypothesis, since lower baseline stores should leave more room for a supplementation effect.
123Best Replication
The largest and most rigorously designed study to directly test this - preregistered, double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover, with roughly half of its 123 participants vegetarian - found only a small overall cognitive benefit from creatine, and explicitly reported that vegetarians did not benefit more than omnivores.
This is a genuinely instructive case, not a "vegans were wrong about everything" story: creatine supplementation still shows a modest overall cognitive benefit in the wider literature, and vegetarians do have measurably lower baseline creatine - both of those facts remain true. What didn't survive the better-designed test was the specific, commonly repeated marketing claim that vegans and vegetarians see a proportionally bigger cognitive payoff.

The likely explanation is a distinction that's easy to miss if you're not paying close attention to which tissue is actually being measured. Muscle creatine and brain creatine are not the same pool, and they don't necessarily track each other. Vegetarians clearly do have lower muscle creatine - that part of the original hypothesis was never in dispute and remains true today. But more careful direct measurements of brain creatine content specifically have found it's fairly similar between vegetarians and omnivores, likely because the brain regulates and protects its own creatine supply somewhat independently of what's happening in muscle tissue and diet. If the brain's creatine pool was never as depleted as the muscle-based reasoning assumed, then there was never as much "room to fill" as the original hypothesis implied, and the larger cognitive response some early studies reported may have had more to do with those particular study samples than with vegetarianism itself. None of this means creatine is a bad choice for vegans - if anything, the case for supplementing to correct genuinely lower muscle stores is straightforward and well supported, and we cover the full range of well-evidenced benefits, dosing, and timing in our dedicated creatine guide. What it means is that the specific "vegans get an extra cognitive bonus" pitch should be retired, or at least held much more loosely than it currently is in casual advice.

The Real Win: Algae-Derived DHA and EPA

After two debunkings, it's worth pausing to make sure this article doesn't leave the impression that vegan supplementation is just a graveyard of overturned claims, because that would be its own kind of distortion. There's a genuinely strong, well-evidenced story here too, and it happens to solve a problem that used to have no good vegan answer at all: getting adequate levels of the long-chain omega-3 fats DHA and EPA.

For most of nutrition history, if you wanted DHA and EPA - the forms of omega-3 most directly tied to brain, eye, and cardiovascular health, as opposed to the shorter-chain ALA found in flax and chia - your only real option was fish oil. Vegans were left relying on plant sources of ALA and hoping their bodies would convert enough of it internally. That hope turns out to be largely misplaced: the enzymatic conversion of ALA into EPA and DHA in the human body is well documented to be inefficient, typically converting under 10% of ALA into EPA and 5% or less into DHA in most people, meaning that even generous flaxseed or chia intake leaves most vegans running a meaningful long-chain omega-3 deficit relative to fish-eaters. For decades, this was simply an unsolved gap - the one place where "eat enough plants" genuinely couldn't get you to parity.

A Direct, Bioavailable Source - Not a Workaround

What changed the picture is a detail about where fish actually get their omega-3s from in the first place: they don't make DHA and EPA themselves. Fish accumulate these fats by eating marine microalgae, which are the true original source in the ocean food chain. Algae-oil supplements simply cut out the fish and extract DHA and EPA directly from cultivated algae - the same molecules, from the same ultimate source, without an animal in between. This isn't an inferior substitute requiring a leap of faith the way the spirulina and creatine claims did; it's the same compound, sourced one step earlier in the chain. Multiple studies confirm algal oil reliably raises omega-3 index levels in supplemented individuals, including people starting from a low baseline, at a rate comparable to fish oil itself. For a full breakdown of dosing, EPA-to-DHA ratios, and how to evaluate quality in an omega-3 product - vegan or otherwise - see our complete omega-3 guide.

It's worth sitting with why this particular story turned out so differently from the first two. Spirulina's B12 claim was built on a measurement artifact that better chemistry eventually exposed. The creatine-cognition claim was built on a reasonable but ultimately unconfirmed extrapolation from a different tissue than the one that mattered. Algae-oil omega-3, by contrast, isn't an extrapolation or a workaround at all - it's the literal original source of the nutrient, delivered with one fewer step in between. That's a meaningfully different kind of claim, and it's exactly the kind of distinction worth training yourself to notice before believing the next confident nutrition claim you come across, vegan or otherwise.

The Unglamorous Basics That Still Deserve Attention

None of the myth-busting or myth-confirming above changes what the actual, boring backbone of vegan supplementation still looks like, and it's worth naming plainly rather than letting the more interesting stories above crowd it out. B12 supplementation remains completely non-negotiable - not "worth considering," not "probably a good idea," but the one nutrient where going without a real supplement or reliably fortified food is, per the research cited above, running a genuine and well-documented health risk. Vitamin D is a related, if less absolute, concern: most people, vegan or not, run low, and vegans historically faced an extra wrinkle because the more effective D3 form was traditionally sourced from lamb's wool lanolin or fish, leaving vegan products stuck with the less efficiently converted D2 form. That gap has meaningfully narrowed with the wider availability of lichen-derived D3, which is both genuinely vegan and the more effective form - we go through the full D2-versus-D3 debate, dosing ranges, and why experts still disagree on the ideal target level in our vitamin D3 and K2 guide.

Zinc rounds out the list of nutrients that deserve ongoing attention specifically because of how plant-based diets are structured, not because of any myth or marketing story - phytates in grains and legumes bind zinc and reduce its absorption, which is a real, mechanistic reason vegan zinc status warrants more attention than it typically gets, independent of total dietary zinc intake looking adequate on paper. Our zinc guide covers the absorption mechanics and dosing in more depth. Iron and iodine round out the list of nutrients worth periodic attention on a plant-based diet, for similar structural reasons - plant-based (non-heme) iron absorbs less efficiently than the heme iron found in meat, and iodine intake can run low without deliberate use of iodized salt or seaweed in moderation, given how few plant foods reliably contain it.

What ties all of this together, and what the two debunked myths above illustrate from opposite directions, is that vegan nutrition doesn't require heroic effort or exotic superfoods - it requires accuracy about which specific gaps are real and which supposed shortcuts are illusory. Spirulina isn't a shortcut around B12; it's a detour that can make things worse. The extra creatine bonus wasn't a hidden vegan advantage; it was a measurement that didn't hold up to a better study. And algae-oil omega-3 isn't a consolation prize for not eating fish; it's the actual source of the nutrient, arrived at more directly. Getting the details right, nutrient by nutrient, does more for long-term health than any single "vegan superfood" claim ever will.

A Practical Checklist for Vegan Supplementation in 2026

What Actually Deserves Your Attention

✓ Take a real B12 supplement, consistently - this remains the single most non-negotiable item on any vegan supplementation list, with no reliable algae-based substitute.
✓ Consider algae-oil DHA/EPA over flaxseed or chia oil if long-chain omega-3 status matters to you - the conversion math genuinely favors the direct algae source.
✓ Look for lichen-derived vitamin D3 specifically rather than D2 if you want the more effective D form in a genuinely vegan product - this option has become more widely available.
✓ Keep an eye on zinc, iron, and iodine status periodically - not because of any myth, but because plant-based absorption mechanics for these three are genuinely less efficient than for their animal-source equivalents.
✗ Don't rely on spirulina, chlorella, or seaweed as your B12 source - at best inconsistent, at worst actively counterproductive if combined with real B12 supplementation.
✗ Don't expect a bigger cognitive boost from creatine specifically because you're vegan - the best available evidence doesn't support that specific claim, even though creatine itself remains a reasonable, well-studied supplement for vegans given lower baseline dietary intake.
